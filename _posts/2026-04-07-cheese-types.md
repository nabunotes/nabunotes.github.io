Cheese is one of the oldest preserved foods, and its variety is staggering. Two useful ways to understand it: by **texture/hardness**, and by **named variety**.

## By Texture and Hardness

This is the technical classification, based on moisture content and the aging process. The drier the cheese, the harder it becomes.

- **Fresh** — unaged, high moisture, mild and milky. Examples: mozzarella, ricotta, cottage cheese.
- **Soft-ripened** — ripened from the outside in by surface mold, creamy interior. Examples: brie, camembert.
- **Washed-rind** — rind is regularly washed in brine or alcohol during aging, producing pungent aroma. Examples: limburger, taleggio.
- **Semi-soft** — moderate moisture, mild to medium flavor. Examples: havarti, muenster.
- **Semi-hard** — pressed curd, lower moisture, firm texture. Examples: gouda, edam, young cheddar.
- **Hard** — aged longer, drier, more complex. Examples: aged cheddar, gruyère, manchego.
- **Extra-hard** — very low moisture, granular texture, often grated. Examples: parmesan, pecorino romano.
- **Blue** — mold (*Penicillium*) introduced inside the cheese, creating blue-green veins and sharp flavor. Examples: gorgonzola, roquefort, stilton.

The transition from fresh to extra-hard is essentially a journey of losing water. Fresh mozzarella is ~55% water; parmesan is closer to 30%.

## By Named Variety

Named varieties differ in how the curd is treated during production. Here are five of the most widely consumed:

- **Cheddar** (England) — the curd goes through *cheddaring*: slabs are stacked and flipped repeatedly to expel whey and build acidity, then milled, salted, and pressed. Flavor ranges from mild to sharp depending on age.

- **Mozzarella** (Italy) — *pasta filata* (stretched curd): the curd is heated in hot water and pulled repeatedly, aligning proteins into long elastic strands. Eaten fresh with little to no aging.

- **Gouda** (Netherlands) — uses a *washed-curd* technique where hot water is added to rinse away lactose, reducing acidity and producing a sweeter, creamier result. Often wax-coated during aging to control moisture.

- **Parmesan / Parmigiano-Reggiano** (Italy) — curd is cooked at high heat, broken into tiny granules, packed into large wheel molds, and aged 12–36 months. Long aging produces crystalline deposits and an intense nutty, savory flavor.

- **Brie** (France) — surface mold (*Penicillium camemberti*) is applied to the young cheese and ripens it from outside in over several weeks, creating the white rind and a creamy, almost runny interior.

## Country of Origin

Many cheeses carry strong regional identity for three reasons:

- **Terroir** — the flavor of milk reflects what animals eat and the local climate. Alpine grasses produce different milk than Dutch lowland pastures.
- **Tradition** — recipes and techniques developed locally over centuries and passed down through generations.
- **Legal protection** — names like *Parmigiano-Reggiano*, *Roquefort*, and *Gruyère AOP* are protected designations that can only be used by producers from the specific region.

| Country | Known for |
|---|---|
| England | Cheddar, Stilton, Cheshire, Gloucester |
| France | Brie, Camembert, Roquefort, Comté, Munster |
| Italy | Parmesan, Mozzarella, Gorgonzola, Pecorino, Ricotta |
| Netherlands | Gouda, Edam |
| Switzerland | Gruyère, Emmental, Appenzeller, Raclette, Sbrinz |
| Greece | Feta |
| Spain | Manchego, Idiazábal |
| Denmark | Havarti, Danbo |

Cheeses like cheddar and gouda are now produced globally, but without the regional conditions and traditional methods, purists consider them *cheddar-style* rather than the real thing — similar to how sparkling wine made outside Champagne cannot legally be called Champagne.
